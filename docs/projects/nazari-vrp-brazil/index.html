Vehicle Routing with REINFORCE: A Zero-Shot Test on Real Delivery Data

Domain: Combinatorial Optimization Industry: Logistics & Supply Chain Keywords: Vehicle Routing Problem, REINFORCE, Attention Model, Zero-Shot Transfer, Olist Brazil Updated: Jul 2026

TL;DR

Reproducing Nazari et al. (2018)’s attention-based CVRP policy shows that the REINFORCE baseline is an architectural choice, not a tuning knob — a greedy baseline collapses at VRP20 while a frozen (Kool) baseline converges to a 19.5% gap against OR-Tools, in exchange for a ~3,700x faster solve. Tested zero-shot on real São Paulo delivery data, that gap triples to 61.3% — a direct measurement of what training on synthetic, uniformly-distributed customers costs once the model meets a real city.

Why routing gets hard fast

A vehicle routing problem is deceptively easy to state: given a depot and a list of customers with known demand, find efficient routes for a capacity-limited fleet that visits everyone exactly once. It’s also combinatorially brutal — a 20-customer instance already has more than 10^17 possible route orderings, far beyond what an exact solver can search directly. Classical solvers handle this by pruning that space, trading time for optimality. Nazari’s approach makes the opposite trade: it doesn’t aim to find the optimal route, only a good enough one, almost instantly. The gain isn’t solution quality — it’s inference time, and that’s the trade-off this project measures directly.

This project reproduces the attention-based policy from Nazari et al. (2018) end to end — architecture, REINFORCE training loop, and baseline variants — evaluates it at two problem sizes against OR-Tools as a classical baseline, and closes with a zero-shot test on real delivery data from São Paulo.

The baseline choice is architectural, not a hyperparameter

The model is a policy trained with REINFORCE, and REINFORCE needs a baseline — a reference value subtracted from the reward to compute how much better (or worse) a given route was than expected. Four variants were trained on VRP10 under identical conditions: no baseline at all, an exponential moving average of past rewards, a greedy baseline (the model’s own deterministic tour), and the Kool baseline (Kool et al., 2019) — a frozen copy of the model, updated only when a statistical test confirms real improvement.

Baseline Gap% vs OR-Tools
No baseline 84.7%
EMA 14.8%
Greedy 13.1%
Kool 17.1%

Gap% based on 32 validation instances.

At VRP10, the problem is small enough that greedy and Kool land at essentially the same quality — the gap between them is within sampling noise (based on 32 validation instances). Only “no baseline” is a clear outlier. That result would suggest baseline choice barely matters, except it doesn’t hold at the next size up.

Baseline Gap% vs OR-Tools
Greedy 179.4%
Kool 19.5%

Gap% based on 32 validation instances.

At VRP20, greedy collapses — to a policy worse than random routing. The reason is structural: the policy and its own baseline share the same weights, so the moment the policy improves, the baseline catches up with it, and the advantage signal that drives learning goes to zero before anything useful is learned. Kool’s frozen copy breaks that feedback loop, producing the classic phase-transition curve — a long flat stretch, then a sharp drop once the policy has pulled far enough ahead of its (stale) reference to get a real gradient. Greedy and Kool aren’t two points on the same curve; one of them simply doesn’t converge once the problem is big enough to matter.

What a suboptimal route looks like

Numbers say Kool’s VRP20 policy is 19.5% behind OR-Tools. Plotting the actual routes shows what that gap looks like on the ground.

A single vehicle makes multiple trips from the depot, reloading between each; every color is one trip.

The model has no lookahead: at each step it only sees the current state — where the vehicle is, which customers are still unserved, how much capacity is left — and picks whichever next customer looks best right now, without simulating the next 5 or 10 steps. It’s a one-step, autoregressive policy, not a planner. That’s exactly what produces the X: the model can head toward a distant customer because it looks like a good local move, with no way to notice that the choice will force it to double back across ground it’s already covered. OR-Tools avoids this because it searches the whole route before committing, routing along the perimeter of each cluster instead; Nazari has no such guarantee. It’s the same trade-off in miniature that explains the whole project — a policy that decides fast and locally will occasionally produce a trip a human dispatcher would immediately flag as backwards.

Speed vs. quality: the actual trade-off

None of this matters without weighing it against what you get in return. OR-Tools searches for up to 30 seconds per instance; Nazari runs a full batch in a single forward pass.

Problem Nazari (greedy) OR-Tools (GLS) Speedup Gap%
VRP10 ~2 ms/instance ~5 s/instance ~2,500x 17.1%
VRP20 ~8 ms/instance ~30 s/instance ~3,700x 19.5%

A ~20% longer tour for a ~3,700x faster solve. For dispatch systems making hundreds of routing calls in real time — or simulating thousands of scenarios where near-optimal is good enough — that trade often wins.

Zero-shot on São Paulo

Every result above comes from customers scattered uniformly inside a unit square — a convenient synthetic distribution, and nothing like a real city. To measure that gap directly, I built VRP20 instances from the Olist Brazilian e-commerce dataset: ~15,500 São Paulo orders, geocoded via postal-code prefix, split into 5 natural delivery zones by K-means, with order weight mapped into the same demand range the model was trained on.

Running the VRP20 Kool model zero-shot — no retraining — against 32 real-customer instances sampled from each zone:

Cluster Model OR-Tools Gap%
C1 (Leste) 5.648 3.644 55.0%
C2 (Norte) 5.535 3.996 40.0%
C3 (Sul) 5.970 3.395 79.4%
C4 (Oeste) 5.294 3.307 61.4%
C5 (Centro-Sul) 6.043 3.572 70.7%
Overall 5.698 3.583 61.3%

The gap triples, from 19.5% on synthetic uniform instances to 61.3% on real customers. That’s the actual finding: São Paulo’s dense, unevenly clustered geography is a structurally different problem from the one the model was trained on, and the cost of that mismatch is measurable, not hand-wavy. Fittingly, the zone closest to a uniform spread (C2) has the smallest gap; the densest zone (C3) has the largest.

What this shows

The interesting parts of this project are the two things a straight reimplementation wouldn’t surface on its own. First, the baseline is an architectural decision, not a tuning knob — it’s invisible at VRP10 and decisive at VRP20. Second, a 19.5% gap on the training distribution and a 61.3% gap on real customers are both true at once, and the difference between them is a precise measurement of what “trained on synthetic data” actually costs once it meets a real city.

Closing the second gap — training on São Paulo’s own spatial distribution via KDE, and scaling further to VRP50 — is the next phase of this work.
